The question most business leaders are asking about Taiwan is the wrong one. They ask: "Will China invade?" It is the wrong frame because it focuses on the most extreme, most telegraphed, and paradoxically least immediately disruptive scenario. The more useful question — the one that determines your actual business exposure — is this: "At what point does Taiwan Strait instability affect our operations, regardless of whether a shot is ever fired?"

The answer, for a growing number of companies, is: it already has.

---

## The Situation as of Early 2026

Cross-strait tensions have been building systematically. In December 2025, the People's Liberation Army conducted "Justice Mission 2025," a live-fire exercise of unprecedented scale that disrupted over 857 international flights and 100,000 travelers in a single operation — the closest live-fire activity ever recorded near Taiwan.

PLA military activities in the Taiwan Strait are now an almost daily occurrence. The Council on Foreign Relations' 2026 Preventive Priorities Survey rates a severe cross-strait crisis as having a 50% or higher probability of occurring within the next 12 months.

The Taiwan Strait Risk Report puts the probability of a Chinese invasion at 30% over the next five years — and the probability of an air and naval blockade at 60%.

These are not fringe assessments. They are mainstream expert opinion.

---

## Why Taiwan Is Different From Every Other Geopolitical Risk

Most supply chain risks are substitutable. If a port closes, you find another port. If a supplier is disrupted, you qualify a backup. Taiwan is different because of one word: TSMC.

Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company controls approximately 72% of the global advanced semiconductor foundry market. The chips that power your iPhone, your data center, your car's electronics, your medical devices — the overwhelming majority of the world's most advanced semiconductors are fabricated on a single island in a contested strait.

Bloomberg Economics estimates that a full-scale disruption of Taiwan's semiconductor production would cost the global economy approximately $10 trillion in the first year — roughly 10% of global GDP, exceeding the impact of the 2008 financial crisis.

But here is what matters for your business planning: you do not need an invasion or a blockade for Taiwan risk to materially affect you. You need only sustained uncertainty to push insurance premiums higher, cause shipping lines to reprice risk, and prompt your customers and suppliers to start making contingency decisions that affect your orders, your lead times, and your pricing.

That process is already underway.

## Three Scenarios for the Taiwan Strait Through 2026

**Scenario A — Managed Friction Continues (Probability: 45%)**

China maintains its current posture of elevated military pressure without crossing into overt kinetic conflict. The status quo is deeply uncomfortable but stable. Semiconductor supply chains function normally. The primary business impact is elevated uncertainty premiums in insurance and finance, and continued pressure on companies to accelerate supply chain diversification.

*Business implication:* Begin qualification of alternative semiconductor suppliers now. Increase safety stock on Taiwan-sourced components. The cost of doing nothing is rising.

**Scenario B — Controlled Escalation, Partial Blockade (Probability: 35%)**

A miscalculation or deliberate escalation triggers a functional quarantine of Taiwan. Insurance underwriters designate the Strait a formal war-risk zone. TSMC production continues but semiconductor logistics are severely disrupted for 30-90 days.

*Business implication:* Companies with just-in-time semiconductor dependencies face acute production halts within weeks. Know your inventory position now, not when the crisis hits.

**Scenario C — Acute Crisis, Kinetic Conflict (Probability: 20%)**

A full military operation triggers the $10 trillion economic shock scenario. TSMC production halts. Global technology, automotive, defense, and consumer electronics supply chains collapse simultaneously.

*Business implication:* No reactive measure taken after this scenario materializes will be sufficient. The only effective response is preparedness built before the crisis.

## What Prudent Business Leaders Are Doing Right Now

The companies that will navigate Taiwan Strait risk most effectively are not the ones with the best crisis response plans — they are the ones building resilience into their operations today, during the window of relative stability that still exists.

That means mapping your semiconductor dependency honestly. It means understanding which of your suppliers have their own Taiwan exposure. It means having a conversation with your insurance broker about coverage in a Taiwan disruption scenario. And it means building the flexibility into your product designs and supplier relationships that would allow you to respond if the situation deteriorates.

The window to do that work is open. It will not stay open indefinitely.
