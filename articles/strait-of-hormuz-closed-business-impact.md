This is not a drill. This is not a hypothetical scenario analysis. As of this morning, March 2, 2026, the Strait of Hormuz — the narrow waterway through which 20% of the world's seaborne oil supply flows — has been effectively shut down following joint US-Israeli strikes on Iran launched February 28.

Major shipping carriers have suspended all bookings for sailings into the Persian Gulf. Dubai, Abu Dhabi, and Doha airports are closed or severely restricted. Air freight rates have spiked 400% in 48 hours on affected corridors. Seven of the world's largest maritime insurance mutuals have announced they will automatically terminate risk coverage for ships entering the Persian Gulf as of March 5.

If your business touches energy, pharmaceuticals, electronics, agriculture, or any supply chain that transits between Asia and Europe, this affects you — whether you know it yet or not.

Here is how to think about it.

---

## What Is Actually Happening

The escalation began February 28 when US and Israeli forces launched coordinated strikes on Iranian targets. Iran responded with retaliatory missile and drone activity across the Gulf region, and the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps broadcast warnings prohibiting vessel passage through the Strait of Hormuz. The result has been a near-total suspension of commercial maritime traffic through one of the world's most critical chokepoints.

Unlike the Red Sea disruption of 2024-2025 — where vessels could reroute around the Cape of Good Hope, adding time and cost but maintaining flow — the Strait of Hormuz has no viable maritime alternative. There is no detour. When Hormuz is blocked, the oil, LNG, and containerized cargo that transits it simply stops moving.

The immediate downstream effects are already cascading. Qatar's LNG exports — a critical source of energy for Europe since the reduction in Russian gas — have halted. Air freight networks are in chaos, with airspace restrictions across Iran, Iraq, Kuwait, Israel, Bahrain, UAE, and Qatar removing a substantial share of global transhipment capacity.

## Three Scenarios From Here

**Scenario A — Short Disruption, Rapid De-escalation (Probability: 40%)**

A ceasefire or back-channel agreement is reached within 7-14 days. Shipping resumes with elevated war-risk premiums. Freight rates remain elevated for 60-90 days as carriers reposition vessels and work through backlogs. Energy prices spike then partially normalize as strategic reserves absorb immediate demand. Business impact is significant but bounded.

*What to watch for:* Omani or Qatari diplomatic mediation announcements. US carrier group repositioning signals. Iranian IRGC public statements shifting in tone.

**Scenario B — Prolonged Conflict, Partial Normalization (Probability: 40%)**

The conflict extends beyond 30 days. The Strait reopens partially but with persistent security risk premiums. War-risk insurance remains extremely expensive or unavailable for some operators. Global freight rates stabilize at a significantly higher base. Energy prices remain elevated, feeding into a broad inflationary pulse across logistics-sensitive industries.

*What to watch for:* Insurance underwriters' formal war-zone designations. OPEC+ emergency meetings. US Congressional authorization debates.

**Scenario C — Extended Closure, Structural Disruption (Probability: 20%)**

The conflict deepens or widens. The Strait remains functionally closed for 60+ days. Global energy markets enter acute crisis. Fertilizer exports are severely disrupted, affecting agricultural supply chains into late 2026. The inflationary and supply shock combines with existing tariff uncertainty to produce a significant global economic contraction.

*What to watch for:* Tanker sinking incidents. Iranian mining of shipping lanes. Expansion of conflict to Saudi Arabian infrastructure.

## What Your Business Should Do This Week

**If you have inventory in transit through the Gulf:** Contact your freight forwarder immediately. Understand where your cargo is and what your options are for alternative routing or temporary storage. Do not assume your insurer covers the current situation — call them today.

**If you rely on Gulf-sourced energy inputs:** Activate your contingency fuel or feedstock plans now. Build safety stock where you can. Review your supplier contracts for force majeure provisions.

**If you have contracts dependent on delivery timelines:** Notify counterparties proactively. Review your own force majeure rights. Document everything.

**If you have no direct exposure:** Do not assume you are insulated. Rising oil prices, higher freight rates, and tighter air cargo capacity will affect input costs and logistics timelines across virtually every industry over the next 60-90 days.
