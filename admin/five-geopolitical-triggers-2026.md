Most business planning models treat geopolitical risk as a footnote. It appears in the appendix, after the financial projections, as a qualitative caveat: "geopolitical uncertainty may affect results." This is not risk management. It is a liability disclaimer dressed up as analysis.

The business leaders who will outperform their peers in 2026 are the ones who understand that geopolitical risk is no longer a tail event. It is the operating environment. The Council on Foreign Relations' 2026 Conflict Risk Assessment found that twenty-eight of the thirty conflict scenarios it monitors have a 50% or higher probability of occurring within the next twelve months.

Here are the five triggers with the highest probability of materially disrupting business operations in North America, Europe, and Asia-Pacific this year — and what you should be doing about each one.

---

## Trigger 1: Middle East Escalation — The Hormuz Chokepoint

**Current status: Active crisis as of March 2, 2026.**

This is no longer a scenario to plan for — it is a situation to manage. US-Israeli strikes on Iran launched February 28 have triggered retaliatory action across the Gulf region and functional closure of the Strait of Hormuz. Twenty percent of the world's seaborne oil supply transits Hormuz. Unlike the Red Sea, there is no alternative route.

**Who is exposed:** Any business with energy-linked input costs, pharmaceutical companies dependent on Gulf air routes, technology companies with just-in-time semiconductor logistics, and any company with operations in the Gulf region.

**Probability of material business impact in 2026: 85%**

---

## Trigger 2: Taiwan Strait — The Semiconductor Chokepoint

**Current status: Elevated tension, daily military incidents.**

Taiwan produces approximately 72% of the world's advanced semiconductors. Expert consensus puts the probability of a severe cross-strait crisis in 2026 at or above 50%. You do not need a war for this to affect your business — sustained uncertainty alone is sufficient to trigger insurance repricing and supply chain disruption.

**Who is exposed:** Technology companies, automotive manufacturers, medical device makers, defense contractors, and any business whose products contain advanced chips.

**Probability of material business impact in 2026: 60%**

---

## Trigger 3: Russia-NATO Escalation — The European Energy and Trade Shock

**Current status: War ongoing, escalation risk elevated.**

The Russia-Ukraine war has now extended into its fourth year. CFR rates the probability of armed clashes between Russia and NATO member states in 2026 at 50% or higher. European companies are operating in an environment of persistently higher energy costs, fragmented supply chains, and the possibility of sudden acute escalation.

**Who is exposed:** Energy-intensive manufacturers with European operations, companies with significant European revenue, businesses sourcing from Eastern European suppliers.

**Probability of material business impact in 2026: 55%**

---

## Trigger 4: US Trade Policy Volatility — The Tariff Uncertainty Tax

**Current status: Ongoing and unpredictable.**

Tariff policy has become a source of persistent, unpredictable cost and complexity for companies with any international supply chain exposure. The challenge is not just the tariffs themselves — it is the velocity of change and the difficulty of planning around policy that can shift significantly within a single news cycle. Companies are paying an "uncertainty tax" in the form of higher inventory buffers and less efficient planning cycles.

**Who is exposed:** Any company with supply chains that cross US borders — which includes the vast majority of mid-market manufacturers, importers, and technology companies.

**Probability of material business impact in 2026: 70%**

---

## Trigger 5: Emerging Market Political Instability — The Hidden Supply Chain Exposure

**Current status:** Multiple flashpoints across Latin America, Sub-Saharan Africa, and Southeast Asia.

This is the risk that most business leaders systematically underestimate because it is diffuse rather than concentrated. There is no single "emerging market crisis" to monitor. Instead, there is a constellation of political instability risks in countries that may not be prominent in your business planning but quietly sit in your supply chain through sub-tier suppliers, raw material sources, or logistics routes.

**Who is exposed:** Companies with complex, multi-tier supply chains, particularly in consumer goods, apparel, electronics components, and raw materials.

**Probability of material business impact in 2026: 45%**

---

## The Common Thread

Look across these five triggers and a pattern emerges. In each case, the companies that will be most severely affected are not those facing the greatest objective exposure — they are those who never mapped their exposure in the first place.

You cannot manage a risk you do not understand. And understanding your geopolitical exposure requires more than reading the news. It requires structured analysis: mapping your supply chain against a risk framework, stress-testing your business model against specific scenarios, and building early warning indicators into your operational monitoring that give you time to respond.

That work takes a few hours. The cost of not doing it can be measured in millions.
